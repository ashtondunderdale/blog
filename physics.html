A practical analysis of teleportation

DECEMBER 2, 2023 | 7 min read ASHTON DUNDERDALE


Teleportation is one of, if not the most well known sci-fi (I use the term sci-fi lightly here) concept and its realisation would mark an immeasurable leap forward for the human race. However, it is not just as simple as moving from one place to another instantaneously. There are many ways this technology could develop, from quantum entanglement, wormholes, or even molecular reconstruction (it is much more philosophical than you think), and quantum particles must still obey the laws of physics to teleportation speed is limited to just under the speed of light.

Let's say for instance, you have some sort of ‘particle transfer device’ in which you push a button and it transfers a collection of atoms from one place to another. For this to occur, a few things will take place; the atoms being transported will have to be deconstructed, and then reassembled in another location in time and space. The question that most raise about this is that if you were to disassemble and reassemble in a different time and location - would it still be the same you? What if the you that was deconstructed had died, and the new you is some type of clone that thinks it is you since it would have your memories. This is largely due to the ‘no cloning’ rule which states that quantum states cannot be duplicated, therefore the consciousness that is the new you would not be you.

A side note: in the context of traditional teleportation, the person being teleported would remain unaware of their teleportation as the memory of them being teleported technically never exists given that the teleportation occurs prior to the reconstruction of matter, representing the non-teleportative state.


Another example: quantum teleportation. A method of transferring information from a sender to a receiver through the use of quantum entanglement, aka ‘spooky action at a distance’. Quantum entanglement describes the idea that particles can share spatial proximity, effectively ‘connecting’ the particle's information, or quantum state; this rule remains true despite any factor of distance. This is slightly different from traditional teleportation as it does not teleport objects per se but instead the quantum information, which is arguably just as useful. Unfortunately, the laws of quantum mechanics dictate that once observed, it will change - we also only have to look at one of the particles to know its state (quantum particles still have to behave in accordance with the laws of physics, such as travelling below the speed of light). Teleportation is a way off with this method and quantum entanglement has much more practical applications within internet security as anyone who observes the particles will change its information.

The object that is being teleported has what is called a quantum ‘state’, which contains the quantum information of all of the particles that make up the object. This can be copied and transferred in theory, however it is not very easy to accurately measure the quantum state of a particle exactly due to the uncertainty principle - the position and momentum of a particle cannot be measured simultaneously (if measuring the state of one particle is difficult, imagine measuring the entire collection of particles within a human). To extract the position of a particle, we can essentially ‘shoot’ a photon (light particle) at the quantum particle and measure the collection of light, but in turn losing the information about the momentum of the particle. Quantum entanglement can be particularly useful here as we can measure the spin of one particle will determine the spin of the second, essentially altering its entangled partner with traversal of distance. The practical uses of this are limited in terms of the number of particles we can teleport, however it can have major advancements in quantum encryption and data security.

Quantum teleportation, particularly with a substantial amount of particles, presents a plethora of obstacles and a lot of things that could go very wrong when performing a quantum teleportation, especially. Quantum interference: at a quantum level, due to the Pauli Exclusion Principle, particles cannot occupy the same quantum state. Any attempt to contravene this principle by forcing particles to coexist in the same space may result in an unpredictable behaviour or large energy release. To mitigate, employ a highly precise and advanced spatial mapping technique. This will ensure the teleportation destination is meticulously selected and will avoid particle overlap - this would certainly recruit the use of a quantum computing algorithm to calculate and control the states during teleportation

In addition, considering our planet's constant movement at approximately 1,000mph through the void of space introduces another critical factor: velocity. Neglecting the velocity component during teleportation could result in finding oneself in the midst of an exceedingly cold vacuum. In such a scenario, your consciousness would fade in 10 seconds and your life within 60.


So, we now know that teleportation is a theoretical feasibility, despite the impracticality of teleporting the entire quantum state of a human being, but there is the philosophical debate of whether it should even be allowed. Questions surrounding individual identity, the nature of consciousness, and the implications of deconstructing and reconstructing a person's quantum state, or does it nullify the existence and uniqueness of human consciousness altogether.

Identity is a way of describing you and all of your particles and their state required to make you. Since all of your atoms are constantly being replaced and renewed, you are in a constant state of movement, it is all you are and all you ever were and what it has led up to - now. That said, quantum teleportation renders your identity, and consciousness for that matter, meaningless. Being teleported is the act of being dematerialised, in other words you are temporarily clinically dead. To teleport willingly is to commit suicide, you are not reborn you are simply reassembled. There is no difference between a pile of broken bits of porcelain and the same atoms but rearranged to form the shape of a mug, it is just an entity, as are you. You can repair the mug over and over again and achieve what seems to be the same mug, yet in an entirely different state, body and soul simply cannot be maintained in a scenario such as this one.

Perhaps, there exists something different about consciousness, that it would carry its identity even after deconstruction - an elusive spark that sets it apart from other things in the universe. Is it truly that extraordinary?

We simply do not know.
